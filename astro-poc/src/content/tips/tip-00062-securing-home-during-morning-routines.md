The period between approximately 6 AM and 8 AM creates a specific vulnerability window that is often overlooked. An occupant who is awake but isolated — in the bathroom, kitchen, or another room away from the front of the house — may not hear a forced entry for several seconds, by which time an intruder has already moved into the home. Criminals who observe a household can identify this pattern and time their approach accordingly, knowing the occupant is unlikely to be positioned near the door. Elderly residents living alone are especially exposed: the gap between breach and confrontation is compressed, and the ability to retreat, barricade, or raise an alarm quickly is limited by both distance and reaction time. Family members and trusted contacts are often the most effective first line of warning in these situations — a missed morning call carries weight that a locked door alone cannot always provide.

**Steps to follow:**

- Maintain the same locked-door protocol in the morning that you apply at night — do not unlock exterior doors during early morning routines until you are dressed and alert.
- Install a contact alarm or door chime on all entry points so any door opening triggers an audible alert regardless of where you are in the house.
- If you are an elderly family member living alone, agree on a fixed morning check-in time with a relative — a missed call or message by that time should prompt a welfare visit, not a follow-up call.
- Keep a mobile phone within reach during your morning routine; do not leave it charging in the bedroom while you move to another part of the house.
- If you hear an unexpected sound while inside a closed room such as a bathroom, do not open the door to investigate — call 999 or a family member from within that room first.
- If you have elderly relatives who live alone, initiate the check-in system proactively — do not wait for them to ask for it.
