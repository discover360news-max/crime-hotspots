A home with a single occupant is a specific type of target — multiple armed men who force entry in the evening understand that no second person is present to raise an alarm, coordinate a response, or create any deterrent. The absence of witnesses inside removes the natural friction that comes from having others nearby, and criminals who use this method move quickly to establish physical control before any rescue response can begin. Being overpowered by a group is a condition that cannot be reversed by resistance; the only effective protective layer is reducing the conditions that make the attack easy to plan and execute. A combination of behavioural habits and visible security measures can shift the risk profile of a solo-occupied home substantially.

**Steps to follow:**

- Avoid patterns that signal to outside observers that you are consistently home alone — vary your schedule, and arrange for a trusted contact or neighbour to call or check in at irregular intervals when you are alone.
- Activate the same interior lights, sounds, and visible signs of activity whether you are alone or not; a home that looks occupied by one person versus several should be indistinguishable from outside.
- Install a reinforced secondary grille or burglar-proof door at your main entrance with its own deadbolt — forced entry through two secured barriers takes significantly longer and increases the chance of detection before access is gained.
- Designate a lockable interior room as a safe room; keep a charged phone there and move there immediately if you hear a forced entry beginning, rather than investigating the source.
- Inform a trusted contact when you are home alone and agree on a specific check-in time — a missed contact at the agreed time should trigger a welfare call or police request.
- After any incident or attempted entry, report to police in person and note any unfamiliar vehicles or individuals observed near the property in the days prior.
