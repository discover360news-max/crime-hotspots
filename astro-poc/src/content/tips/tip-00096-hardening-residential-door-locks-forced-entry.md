A 47-year-old man broke into a Curepe home by forcing the lock on the eastern front door while the owner was away for approximately four hours. The interior was ransacked and the intruder was found asleep on the bedroom floor when the homeowner returned. The forced entry required no specialised tools and exploited a single weak point — the door lock itself. Standard pin-tumbler locks and hollow-core exterior doors can be defeated quickly with basic force, and even a brief absence creates a usable window if the locks protecting your home cannot withstand it. Most residential burglaries in T&T exploit structural weakness at the point of entry, not failures of alarm or surveillance systems.

**Steps to follow:**

- Fit a deadbolt lock on all exterior doors — a deadbolt extends a solid metal bolt deep into the door frame and is significantly harder to defeat by kicking or manipulation than a spring latch alone.
- Reinforce the door frame around the lock strike plate using a heavy-duty plate secured with 3-inch screws that reach into the wall stud, not just the surface timber; a reinforced frame prevents the door from splitting away from the lock even under heavy force.
- Replace hollow-core exterior doors with solid-core or steel doors — hollow-core doors can be punched or kicked through regardless of the quality of the lock fitted to them.
- Do not leave spare keys outside the property under mats, above door frames, or inside planters; if a hidden key is found, it removes every other security measure you have installed.
- Lock every exterior door — including side and rear doors — even when you are leaving for a short period; intruders probe the least visible entry point first, not the front door.
- If a lock or door frame is damaged, replace it promptly; a visibly compromised entry point signals to anyone observing the property that the home is an easy target.
