Having your photos and personal information stolen to run a romance scam is a form of identity theft that affects you even though you are not the one being defrauded — your image is used to extract money from strangers, and when those victims discover the deception, your real contact details may be circulated in retaliation or harassment. In a case reported in Jamaica, a social media influencer discovered her photos had been used to build fake profiles on Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and dating sites. The scammer extracted money from men before blocking them, then circulated the victim's real phone numbers online. Strangers began approaching the victim in public, believing they had been in a relationship with her. The cloning was traced to someone connected to a former partner, and enforcement under Jamaica's cybercrime legislation remained limited. Reducing the availability of high-resolution photos and personal details from your public profiles significantly lowers the chance that your identity can be cloned convincingly.

**Steps to follow:**

- Set your social media accounts — particularly Instagram, Facebook, and TikTok — to private or friends-only; public profiles allow anyone to download your photos in bulk without your knowledge.
- Limit the resolution and volume of face-forward photos that are publicly visible; scammers rely on a large library of images to maintain fake profiles across multiple platforms.
- Conduct a reverse image search of your profile photos using a search engine's image search feature periodically — this can surface fake accounts using your images before the harm escalates.
- If you discover a fake profile using your identity, report it directly to the platform using the impersonation report tool, and simultaneously report it to the JCF Cyber Crimes Unit; document the fake profile with screenshots before reporting in case it is deleted.
- Do not publish your personal phone numbers publicly — use messaging services or platform-specific contact forms; once your number is circulated online, it cannot be retracted.
- If you are being harassed as a result of someone else using your identity, save all evidence and report it under the Cybercrimes Act to your nearest police station — the law provides specific provisions for identity-based harassment.
