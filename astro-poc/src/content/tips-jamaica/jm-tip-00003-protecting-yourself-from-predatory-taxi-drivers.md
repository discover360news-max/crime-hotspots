Boarding a route taxi alone creates a one-on-one situation with the driver — and if the driver has predatory intent, the vehicle itself becomes the mechanism of control. In a March 2026 incident in Spanish Town, a 13-year-old girl boarded a taxi heading to Angels and was instead diverted to Angels Grove Housing Scheme, where the driver locked the vehicle and sexually assaulted her. The tactic relies on two factors: the passenger's unfamiliarity with the route and the driver's ability to control the door locks once the vehicle is moving. Children and young women travelling alone are disproportionately targeted. Recognising a route deviation early — before the vehicle reaches an isolated area — is the most critical window to act.

**Steps to follow:**

- Share your route and the vehicle's licence plate with a trusted contact before boarding — plate numbers are visible from outside the car and take seconds to photograph and send.
- Know the landmarks along your intended route: if the driver takes a turn that does not match the known path, say so immediately, loudly and clearly, and demand to be let out.
- Sit in the back seat when possible — this reduces direct physical access and gives you a few additional seconds to react if the driver's behaviour changes.
- If the vehicle is moving and you feel unsafe, attempt to attract attention: lower the window if possible, activate your phone's emergency feature, or make noise — any public attention creates risk for the perpetrator.
- Prefer licensed JUTC buses or route taxis with visible operator markings for solo journeys; avoid flagging down unmarked or private-looking vehicles, especially when travelling at non-peak hours.
- Report any incident — whether completed or attempted — to the Centre for the Investigation of Sexual Offences and Child Abuse (CISOCA) at 119 or your nearest police station; early reporting supports investigation and may prevent further attacks.
