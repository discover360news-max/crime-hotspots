A locked bedroom door is a last line of defence — but a standard door lock can be forced open in seconds by a determined intruder who has already entered your home. In a March 2026 incident in Golden Run, Manchester, a woman locked her bedroom door before sleeping and woke to find it being forced open by a man who then attempted to rape her. The lock slowed but did not stop the attacker; she was able to resist and cause him to flee. Standard residential interior doors and their hardware are not designed to withstand physical forced entry. A reinforced bedroom door can be the difference between an attacker being stopped at a barrier and reaching you while you sleep.

**Steps to follow:**

- Replace standard interior door locks with a deadbolt or door bar — a bolt that extends into the door frame provides significantly more resistance than a spring latch alone.
- Install a door security bar or jammer (a wedge-style brace placed under the handle) as a secondary measure for overnight use; these are inexpensive and prevent inward-swinging doors from being forced even if the lock fails.
- Ensure your bedroom door frame is solid and screws are secured into the wall stud — a lock is only as strong as the frame it is mounted in; hollow-core doors and short screws in soft wood fail quickly under force.
- Keep a charged mobile phone within reach while you sleep so you can call 119 without having to locate it under pressure.
- If you believe someone has entered your home while you are inside, do not investigate — lock your bedroom door, call 119, and stay on the line until help arrives.
- Report any person who has made threats or been near your property without permission to the Mandeville or local parish police before an incident occurs — JCF can advise on protective measures and document prior contact.
