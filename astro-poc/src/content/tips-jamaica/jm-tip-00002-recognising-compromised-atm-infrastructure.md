ATM theft is not limited to robberies at gunpoint — some incidents involve direct access to the machine's internal cash compartment, bypassing the withdrawal process entirely. In a March 2026 incident in Portmore, St Catherine, a masked individual was captured on CCTV removing approximately $9.5 million from an ATM's cash bin without making a standard transaction. The cash had been loaded months earlier, and the breach went undetected for an extended period. Incidents of this nature suggest either insider knowledge of the machine's service schedule, a compromised lock mechanism, or surveillance of maintenance routines. As an ATM user, signs of physical tampering — broken panels, loose parts around the machine, or unusual devices attached to the card slot — indicate the machine may have been accessed by unauthorised persons and should not be used.

**Steps to follow:**

- Before inserting your card, inspect the ATM for any visible physical damage: cracked or broken panels, loose fascia, unfamiliar devices attached to the card slot or keypad, or unusual items placed near the machine.
- Avoid using ATMs that are isolated, poorly lit, or located in areas without active foot traffic or security — machines in exposed locations in Portmore, Spanish Town, and outer-parish areas carry higher risk.
- If you notice an ATM has been tampered with or is behaving unusually (unexpected prompts, card reader resistance, or mechanical sounds), do not proceed — leave the area and report it to the bank using the contact number displayed on the machine.
- Prefer ATMs inside bank branches or monitored lobbies over standalone kiosks on open streets, especially outside business hours.
- Monitor your account for any deductions you did not initiate and report them to your bank within 24 hours — early reporting improves the chance of recovery and supports investigations.
