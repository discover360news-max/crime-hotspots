Louvre and glass window panes can be removed silently and quickly by a burglar — making them a preferred entry point when an intruder wants to avoid the noise of breaking glass or forcing a door. In a March 2026 incident in Silent Hill District, Manchester, a 21-year-old man removed window panes from a room and stole several items while the homeowner slept just metres away. The homeowner only discovered the breach when they woke and found the windows missing. Standard louvre fittings hold panes in place with minimal mechanical resistance; a burglar who has observed your home's layout and sleeping hours can work through a louvre window in under a minute. The theft in this case cost the homeowner valuables and the cost of replacement — a targeted security fix costs far less.

**Steps to follow:**

- Secure louvre windows with epoxy adhesive applied to the ends of each blade in its housing — this turns removable panes into fixed ones without blocking airflow and costs very little to implement.
- Install security grilles or steel bars on windows that face a fence line, shared yard, or area with limited natural surveillance — particularly on rooms that are used for sleeping.
- Where louvre windows face a public or accessible area, add a secondary internal lock or window latch that prevents the frame from being pushed inward even after the panes are disturbed.
- Do not store high-value items in rooms with louvre or single-pane windows that face outward on ground level — relocate valuables to interior rooms or use a locked container.
- If you are alone at night and your home does not have barred windows, consider a door alarm or vibration sensor as an early warning for tampering — low-cost options are available at hardware stores in Mandeville and other parish centres.
- Report known or suspected burglars in your district to the JCF; the Manchester police area has a history of daytime and overnight residential burglaries and local intelligence aids targeted patrols.
