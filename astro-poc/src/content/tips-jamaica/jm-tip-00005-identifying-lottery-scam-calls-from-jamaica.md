Jamaica-based lottery scam operations are a documented and active criminal industry — Anti-Lottery Scam Unit operations in parishes including Portland regularly uncover organised groups using phones and electronic devices to defraud people abroad, particularly in the United States, Canada, and the United Kingdom. In March 2026, three men from Portland were arrested after searches of their residences yielded devices containing the personal information of overseas victims, along with seized vehicles and over $300,000 in cash. The scam works by convincing victims they have won a prize and must pay fees upfront to release funds — no prize ever arrives. The callers are skilled at building rapport across multiple calls before the financial request comes. Recognising the pattern early eliminates the risk entirely.

**Steps to follow:**

- Treat any unsolicited call or message claiming you have won a lottery, grant, or inheritance — particularly from a Jamaican or unknown overseas number — as a definite scam attempt; legitimate prizes do not require upfront fees.
- Never provide banking details, wire transfers, gift card codes, or personal identification numbers to any caller claiming to administer a prize or government payment.
- If a caller references your name, address, or prior personal details to build credibility, end the call — scammers purchase personal data and use it to appear legitimate.
- Do not engage with the caller beyond ending the call: extended engagement allows them to adjust their approach and increases the risk you will eventually comply.
- Block the number and report it to your phone carrier as a scam call; if you are in Jamaica and receive a tip that a scam operation is active in your community, report it to the JCF Anti-Lottery Scam Unit or call 119.
- Warn family members living abroad — Jamaican diaspora communities in the US, UK, and Canada are among the most frequently targeted groups by these operations.
